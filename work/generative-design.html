The Exploration

When algorithms design better than intuition alone

Generative design had always felt distant — almost like science fiction. The idea that a computer could explore thousands of design permutations simultaneously, optimising for strength, weight, material usage, and manufacturing constraints, felt too abstract to be practical.

This project was the moment that changed. Running a topology optimisation study in SolidWorks Simulation, the software analysed the component under defined load conditions and identified exactly which regions of material are structurally essential — and which can be safely removed without compromising performance.

What I Learned

Speaking the language of machines

The study begins by defining the design space — the maximum volume the part could occupy — along with the load cases, constraints, and manufacturing goals. The algorithm then iteratively removes material from regions that contribute least to structural performance, converging on a geometry that a human designer would rarely arrive at intuitively.

What makes topology optimisation genuinely powerful is that it removes designer bias. There are no assumptions about what the part should look like — only constraints about how it must perform. The result is often organic, almost biological-looking geometry that is simultaneously lighter and stronger than conventional designs.

I am still early in this journey — understanding processes, making mistakes, learning to interpret simulation outputs and translate them into manufacturable designs. But that is exactly how you build real competence. Step by step.
