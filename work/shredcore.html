The Problem

Plastic waste doesn't have to be a dead end

Small workshops, makerspaces, and sustainability-focused creators generate significant plastic waste — failed prints, off-cuts, scrap material — with no practical way to process it on-site. Industrial shredders are too large and too expensive. The gap between "I want to recycle this" and "I have the equipment to do it" is enormous.

ShredCore Tabletop was designed to close that gap — a compact, motor-driven plastic shredder that fits on a workbench and is manufacturable from standard steel sections, off-the-shelf fasteners, and a single-phase motor.

System Breakdown

Every component designed with purpose

🔩
Shredding Chamber
Dual-shaft counter-rotating blade assembly enclosed in a bolted steel housing. Blades are designed for interchangeability and easy replacement.
📥
Angled Feed Hopper
Offset-angle hopper guides material safely into the cutting zone while preventing jamming and keeping hands clear of the blades.
⚙️
Belt & Pulley Drive
Motor torque transmitted via a V-belt and dual-pulley system — providing mechanical advantage, vibration damping, and easy speed ratio adjustment.
📦
Removable Collection Bin
Shredded plastic falls directly into a slide-out steel bin housed within the base frame — making collection clean, fast, and tool-free.
🔲
Acrylic Inspection Cover
Transparent acrylic panel over the shredding chamber allows real-time monitoring of feed material and blade performance without opening the machine.
🏗️
Welded Steel Frame
Structural base frame built from standard steel sections with levelling feet — designed for rigidity under dynamic shredding loads.
Why It Matters

Designed for the circular economy.

This machine is part of a broader vision — one where small-scale makers and workshops can close the loop on their own plastic waste, shredding it back into raw feedstock for reuse in injection moulding or filament extrusion. The design prioritises repairability, standard components, and ease of manufacture.

Design Process

From blank canvas to full assembly

The design process began with defining the core mechanical requirement: a shredding mechanism capable of processing common thermoplastics like PET, HDPE, and ABS. The dual-shaft counter-rotating blade system was selected for its proven effectiveness and relatively simple mechanical arrangement.

Each sub-assembly was modelled separately in SolidWorks and then brought together in a top-level assembly with full mate relationships, ensuring every component fits together as it would in fabrication. Special attention was paid to the drive system — the belt and pulley arrangement was sized to deliver adequate torque at the blade shaft while keeping the motor within a practical power range.

The removable collection bin and acrylic inspection cover reflect a user-centred approach — the machine needs to be practical to operate, not just functional in principle. Both features were added after thinking through how someone would actually use this day-to-day in a workshop environment.

Reflection

A machine that does something real.

"This was one of those projects where the complexity sneaks up on you. What starts as a simple shredder concept quickly becomes a system — drive ratios, blade geometry, frame rigidity, safety access, material flow. Every decision connects to the next. Designing something that could actually be built, and actually solve a problem, is a completely different challenge from designing something that just looks good on screen. This one had to work."
