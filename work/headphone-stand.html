The Challenge

Designing for stability in a dynamic environment

This project explores the application of mechanical design principles and intuitive product thinking in the development of a functional headphone stand built specifically for the corporate desk environment.

A key challenge identified early was the elevated center of gravity, which could lead to instability during everyday interaction — particularly when placing or removing headphones under the subtle drag and pull of daily use.

The Solution

Expanding the footprint, lowering the mass

To address the stability problem, the base was intentionally expanded to increase the support footprint and improve resistance to tipping under dynamic conditions.

Additionally, integrated pockets within the base allow for the placement of supplementary weights, effectively lowering the center of mass and ensuring the stand remains firmly planted on the desk during use.

Reflection

Designing with intention.

"This project pushed me to think deeply about intuitive design — designing with real user behaviour, constraints, creativity and environment in mind. Since the stand is meant for a work desk, stability became a key requirement. It needed to resist drag when placing or removing headphones, without shifting or tipping over. It's always exciting to see how simple ideas evolve when you design with intention."

Design Process

From concept to prototype

The design process began with identifying real-world usage patterns of desk headphone stands — observing how users interact with them under time pressure, the typical forces applied during removal, and the surface conditions of a standard corporate desk.

The floral-lobed base geometry was selected not just for aesthetics, but as a functional decision — maximising footprint area while keeping material usage efficient. The hollow base cavity was then engineered to accept removable weights, giving users the ability to customise stability based on headphone size and weight.

The final design was modelled in SolidWorks and validated through physical prototyping via 3D printing, confirming that the weight-pocket concept performed as intended and that the assembly was manufacturable without support structures.
